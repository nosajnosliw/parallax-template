UI UX INTERACTION DESIGN



PROJECTS

DISCOVERY

Understanding exactly what you are building, why you're building it and what will be used to build it is vital for a rudimentary understanding of who your user will be. Android users aren't always iOS users, neither are their respective designers and developers. An experiential real estate sales center UI demands a different set of attention and external API's than a magazine app or e-commerce site. And sometimes your client will be the only user so building something to meet the user goals is totally dependent on making their every wish come true.

I love the discovery phase because its where ideas hit the fan and congeal into something with the potential for either failure or magic. Discovery with a team is even more exciting because you get to share and learn from each others' unique design thinking, backgrounds and raw unbridled creativity.

SKETCH

Eve's rib. I like to start sketching right away, even if it's during the meeting, even if its just a logo doodle. In fact Discovery and initial sketch almost go hand in hand. This allows me to start to envision the existence of a final product, and also envision people using and breaking it.

I come from a writing background and was always taught to keep a journal always and to write down everything, every idea, every dream. The same applies for design. Here's a little nugget I picked up from the Design Gym, "If you don’t write it down (or draw it) it didn’t happen."

RESEARCH

An extension of the discovery phase is research. I must admit outside of a proper UX role I've yet to do the kind of in depth user observation research that professional research teams engage in but I'm so anxious to jump into this realm. For the time being I spend a lot of time trying to pretend like I'm not staring at people during my train commute while they are frantically swiping and jabbing and playing nose deep in their tablets and phones. One facet that I'm keen on these days is noticing how left handed users use their phones and when and why people use the landscape orientation on their devices.

My one man UX team brand of UX research usually consists of rigorous product comparison and reviews, and personal testing of experiences that are similar to those that I am designing. A little bit of qualitative factual context never hurt either.

KEEP SKETCHING

Reiteration happens...'nuff said. It's alright to not use a pencil this time, and maybe jump to wireframes. Though a recent school of thought (one that I'm almost in league with) would say ditch the frames and head straight to the prototype. A little more fidelity, if time and budget allow, can add more clarity of vision and salience to the design.

PROTOTYPING

One thing about prototyping is it opens the door to learning new software and programming tools. I'm getting better and better with creating 'pixel perfect' psd's in Photoshop but recently have fallen in love with Sketch. The workflow is much easier and frankly 'pixel perfection' takes a lot of time, and the faster we can complete one iteration, the faster it can be reviewed and tested and reiterated if need be. Prototyping also keeps my coding curiosity rev'ed up. I'm currently working through Angular and Javascript tutorials.

TESTING & FEEDBACK

User testing is yet one avenue of UX design that I'm still getting my feet wet with. At AV&C we don't do a lot of A/B and User Testing as our products aren't typically consumer facing. However, we do get a lot of feedback about functionality and design of our products whether it is the UI layout or the speed of interactivity in an addressable LED wall or the blending and mapping of a projection and its impact on the physical experience. This feedback is critical as ultimately delivering what the client desires is pretty much aligned with delivering a UX that allows the users to meet their goals.

Learning and adapting from this feedback surely helps to build the level of empathy for users and in our case helps to navigate client expectations and preempt their anxiety about the complex workflows that are required when building custom interactive and generative software.
